### Section 2: Before You Start

**Header:** What You Need Before Any Installation

*[Image: Prepared installation workspace with tools laid out]*

Every MoShiEx installation starts with the same foundation. A clean surface, the right tools, and a controlled environment. Skipping preparation steps is the most common cause of problems that show up during or after installation.

**Tools Required**

*[Image: PPF installation toolkit — squeegees, slip solution, heat gun, trim tools]*

For PPF installation:
- Slip solution (installation fluid)
- Squeegee with soft edge
- Heat gun or heat lamp
- Trim tool or tuck tool for edges
- Spray bottles (one for slip solution, one for rinse water)
- Clean microfiber towels
- Lint-free gloves

For window film installation:
- Application solution
- Hard card squeegee
- Soft felt-wrapped squeegee or slip sleeve
- Spray bottle
- Sharp cutting tool with fresh blades
- Clean microfiber cloths

**Environment Requirements**

*[Image: Clean, dust-free installation bay with controlled lighting]*

Install in a clean, enclosed space. Dust and airborne particles will embed under the film during application. Once a particle is under the film, it cannot be removed without lifting the film and starting the section again.

Optimal installation temperature is between 15°C and 30°C. Avoid installation in direct sunlight or in cold conditions below 10°C. Cold adhesives are stiffer and harder to position. Hot surfaces can cause the film to tack too quickly.

MoShiEx PPF is designed to perform in varied climates, but installation conditions still affect the process. Read the product data sheet for the specific series being installed, as some variants have climate-specific notes.

### Section 3: PPF Installation

**Header:** PPF Installation Overview

*[Image: Full PPF installation sequence from surface prep to final inspection]*

**Step 1 — Surface Preparation**

*[Image: Clay bar decontamination on vehicle panel]*

Wash the vehicle thoroughly. Remove all road film, wax, and contamination. Follow with a clay bar treatment on all panels to be covered. Clay removes embedded surface contamination that washing alone does not lift. After claying, wipe the panels with an IPA (isopropyl alcohol) panel wipe to remove any remaining residue and degreaser.

Do not touch the cleaned panels with bare hands. Skin oils create invisible contamination that affects adhesion.

**Step 2 — Panel Inspection**

*[Image: Installer inspecting panel under raking light]*

Inspect the paint surface under raking light before applying any film. Look for chips, scratches, or paint defects that need to be addressed before the film goes on. PPF will hold the surface it is applied over. Chips or imperfections under the film will remain visible and are harder to address after the film is in place.

**Step 3 — Cutting and Sizing**

*[Image: Film being cut to pattern or pulled from pre-cut kit]*

Cut the film to the required panel dimensions. Add enough extra material on all sides to allow for stretching and edge tucking. Pre-cut computer patterns are recommended for consistent results, particularly on complex panels with curves and cutouts.

**Step 4 — Film Application**

*[Image: Film being positioned on wet panel with slip solution]*

Wet the panel surface and the film adhesive side with slip solution. This allows the film to be repositioned on the panel before the adhesive locks in. Position the film over the panel with even overlap on all sides.

Working from the center of the panel outward, squeegee the film down onto the surface. Apply steady, even pressure. Move the slip solution toward the edges and out from under the film. Keep the squeegee moving. Stopping in place creates pressure marks.

**Step 5 — Edge Wrapping**

*[Image: Installer using heat gun and tuck tool to wrap film around panel edge]*

Use gentle heat from a heat gun to soften the film at the edges. Heated film stretches and conforms to curves and edges without tearing. Tuck the edge material around the panel lip using a tuck tool. Work in short sections. Apply heat, stretch gently, tuck, and move on. Do not overheat. Overheated film stretches unevenly and can deform.

For Lhote's 1.83m wide format film, edge wrapping on large hoods follows the same technique but requires a second person to hold the opposite edge while the first is being tucked.

**Step 6 — Final Inspection**

*[Image: Installer using raking light to inspect finished installation]*

After the film is down and edges are tucked, inspect the full panel under raking light. Look for:
- Trapped air bubbles (small ones will disappear during curing; large ones need to be addressed now)
- Dust or contamination under the film
- Edge sections that have not seated properly
- Lifting corners or seams

Small bubbles near edges are normal and will cure out. Large bubbles in the center of a panel indicate insufficient squeegeeing during application.

**Step 7 — Cure and Handover**

*[Image: Vehicle in clean bay during cure period, customer handover]*

Allow the vehicle to cure in a clean, covered space for 48 to 72 hours before the customer takes delivery. Advise the customer not to wash the vehicle or expose it to prolonged water contact during this window. Provide the customer with the maintenance guide and register the installation through the MoShiEx electronic warranty platform before handover.

### Section 4: Window Film Installation

**Header:** Window Film Installation Overview

*[Image: Window film installation sequence on a door glass panel]*

**Step 1 — Glass Preparation**

*[Image: Glass being cleaned with ammonia-free cleaner and squeegee]*

Clean the glass thoroughly. Use an ammonia-free glass cleaner and a clean rubber squeegee. Remove all contamination, smears, and any existing film or adhesive residue. The adhesive side of the window film will bond to whatever is on the glass surface. Contamination trapped under window film is permanent once the adhesive sets.

For rear windows with defroster lines, work carefully around the lines with a soft cloth. Do not scrape across defroster lines with hard tools.

**Step 2 — Film Cutting and Sizing**

*[Image: Film being cut to glass dimensions on a cutting table]*

Cut the film to the glass dimensions with enough extra on all edges to allow for trimming after application. If using pre-cut patterns, verify the fit before removing the liner. A mis-cut piece of film cannot be re-used.

**Step 3 — Film Application**

*[Image: Window film being wet-applied to interior glass surface]*

Wet the glass with application solution. Remove the liner from the film and wet the adhesive side with solution. Position the film on the glass with even overlap on all edges. The solution allows repositioning before the adhesive contacts the dry glass.

Starting from the top, use a hard card squeegee to push the solution down and out from under the film. Work in overlapping strokes from top to bottom. Use a slip-sleeved squeegee or felt-wrapped card for areas where a hard card might scratch the film surface.

**Step 4 — Edge Trimming**

*[Image: Film edge being trimmed cleanly against door seal]*

Trim the film edges against the door seals, frame, and glass edge. Use a sharp blade and a straight trim guide where possible. Clean cuts prevent edge lifting over time. A ragged cut edge exposes the adhesive and can catch on door seals as the window operates.

**Step 5 — Final Inspection and Cure**

*[Image: Inspecting finished tinted window in raking light]*

Inspect the installed film for contamination, air pockets, and edge issues. Small water pockets immediately after installation are normal and cure out as the water evaporates through the film. Pockets that remain after two to three weeks indicate poor adhesion and should be addressed.

Advise the customer not to roll the windows down for 48 hours. Provide care instructions at handover.

### Section 5: Common Installation Mistakes

**Header:** What Goes Wrong and How to Avoid It

*[Image: Illustration or photo showing common installation mistakes]*

**Contaminated surface**

Dust, skin oils, or residue left on the panel before film application create bubbles and lifting that show up days or weeks later. Surface preparation is not optional. Every step of the prep sequence exists for a reason.

**Over stretching the film**

Stretching film past its elongation limit causes thinning, distortion, and eventual failure at the stretched point. MoShiEx PPF stretches past 280% before failure, but that is the engineering limit, not a routine operating target. Use heat and proper technique to conform the film rather than relying on stretch alone.

**Rushing the edge tuck**

Edges tucked too fast without enough heat, or tucked too far without working from multiple points simultaneously, tend to lift within days. Apply heat in short bursts, work in sections, and let the film cool slightly before moving to the next section.

**Wrong tools**

Using a hard squeegee without a slip sleeve on PPF will leave scratch marks on the surface coating. Using the wrong blade or a dull blade for window film trimming creates ragged edges. Use tools appropriate for the material.

**Not registering the warranty**

The electronic warranty system protects both the installer and the customer. Installations that are not registered have no warranty record. Register every job before the customer leaves.
